UX Problems
1.	inDrive
a.	There is an unnecessary step when inputting the pick-up and drop-off points. When prompted to enter the pick-up address, you input your location, but once confirmed, the app reverts back to the map view displaying your location. You are then required to manually select "Where to & for how much?" before proceeding - this is a redundant step that interrupts the flow and adds friction to what should be a seamless booking experience.
b.	When you select the 3-bar menu button on the top left, it displays your profile picture and name. However, selecting it reveals driver-facing options asking "How do you want to earn income with us?" rather than your personal profile details. To access your actual profile settings, you must select the small profile picture in the top right corner of the screen. This placement is unintuitive, as most users would not assume that icon leads to additional profile options, making it easy to overlook.
2.	Varisty Vibe
a.	The search function offers three options: All, Freemium, and Premium. The "All" option is redundant - removing it and keeping Freemium and Premium as separate selections would be more effective. Users who are subscribed will naturally select Premium. Given that this is a student-facing app, most students do not bank with Standard Bank, which offers Premium at no cost, and it is unrealistic to expect the majority of students to pay R200 for a discount.
b.	Promotional deals that have reached their buyer limit continue to display on the page beyond their validity. These should be automatically removed once the set buyer count has been reached. Since the promotional link is already connected to the website, an automatic trigger should be implemented to unpublish the deal the moment the limit is hit. Leaving expired promotions visible creates a poor user experience, as students will attempt to claim deals that are no longer available, leading to frustration and a loss of trust in the platform.
3.	Voda Pay
a.	After winning a Prime Video subscription through the app, I discovered that managing or cancelling the subscription can only be done within the app itself. However, the app is cluttered with an overwhelming number of options, making it difficult to locate the relevant setting. If the subscription is not deactivated before the end date, VodaPay will automatically deduct the Amazon Prime fee from your airtime balance each month, which is a significant usability risk given how hard the option is to find.
b.	A similar issue exists with recurring data deals. After registering for a R350 per month data deal - which automatically deducts R350 when airtime exceeding that amount is purchased at month end - there is no clear way to cancel it within the app. The option appeared during registration but provided no indication of how to reverse it. Both issues point to the same core problem: the app allows users to subscribe to or activate services easily, but provides no intuitive or visible path to manage or cancel them.
UI Problems
1.	inDrive
a.	The app's visual design feels outdated, resembling something built in 2017 rather than a modern product. Greater investment in the UI is needed - specifically in the graphics, spacing, colour palette, and overall visual identity. The current colour choices and graphic style are unappealing and may discourage users from engaging with the app before they even explore its features.
b.	Compounding the visual issues, several interactive elements are too small. The profile option in particular is undersized to the point where a user unfamiliar with the app may mistake it for a decorative image rather than a selectable button. This is a direct usability problem - if an element does not look interactive, users will not interact with it. Both issues stem from the same underlying gap: the app lacks a considered, modern design language. Improving the visual hierarchy, scaling up key interactive elements, and refreshing the colour system would significantly improve both the aesthetic appeal and the overall user experience.
2.	Varsity Vibe
a.	The app is overly minimalistic, with tabs that lack text labels entirely. Users are left to interpret icons alone to understand each section - this is not only unintuitive for general users but presents a real accessibility barrier for those with low vision, who rely on descriptive text to navigate effectively.
b.	The tab structure adds further complexity by cascading into a secondary view that compares what is available under Freemium versus Premium. While the intent may be to highlight the value of Premium, this approach can overwhelm users and introduce unnecessary decision fatigue - particularly for users who are neurodivergent, such as those on the autism spectrum, where cluttered comparisons can cause cognitive overload. As previously noted, simply presenting Freemium and Premium as two distinct, clearly labelled options would be far more effective. Users who are subscribed to Premium will select it naturally, without needing a side-by-side breakdown to guide them. Simplifying this structure would improve clarity, reduce friction, and make the app more inclusive for a wider range of users.
3.	Voda Pay
a.	The app is cluttered with an excessive number of small tabs, small font sizes, and no clear categorical structure. While using icon-based tiles is understandable, it becomes confusing without overarching headings that group related options together. A single heading covering all relevant options within a category would significantly reduce visual noise and make navigation more intuitive.
b.	It is evident the app was designed to consolidate multiple services - utility bills, transport tickets, phone and Wi-Fi contracts, and more - into a single platform. The ambition is valid, but the execution needs rethinking. A structure similar to that of established banking apps, such as FNB, would serve this better. FNB manages a comparable breadth of features by organising them under clear, logical categories rather than presenting everything at once. Adopting a similar approach would reduce clutter, improve the user's ability to find what they need quickly, and give the app a more polished and professional feel without sacrificing the range of services it offers.
